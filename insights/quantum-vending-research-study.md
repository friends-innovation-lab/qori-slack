## 📌 Abstract

This research explores whether quantum-enabled vending machines (QVMs) can enhance snack access and social interaction within urban microclimates. Using a six-month mixed-methods approach, the study examined user behavior, neighborhood dynamics, and the metaphysical mystery of probabilistic snack delivery. Findings were, at best, inconclusive—but weirdly promising.

---

## 🔍 Research Questions

1. Can probabilistic snack machines improve snack equity in dense urban areas?
2. Do QVMs increase or decrease community social engagement?
3. What emotional and psychological effects do quantum snack selections have on humans?

---

## 🧭 Methodology

### 1. Field Observation

- **Sites**: Temperateville, Fogtown, Humidopolis  
- **Duration**: 6 months  
- **Subjects**: 45 residents, 6 stray cats, 2 confused urban planners  
- **Tools Used**:
  - Snack sentiment diaries
  - Quantum uncertainty logs
  - Wearable snack sensors

### 2. Simulated Snack Dispersion Modeling

- Built a custom simulation in `snackstream.py` (Python)
- Ran 10,000 Monte Carlo simulations to map snack probabilities across humidity conditions

### 3. Surveys & Interviews

- Likert scale ratings (1–5) on snack satisfaction, confusion, and community snack rituals
- Optional open-ended questions like “How do you *feel* about Schrödinger’s Nachos?”

---

## 📊 Key Findings

| Metric                     | Control Group | QVM Group | Δ (Difference) |
|---------------------------|---------------|-----------|----------------|
| Snack Satisfaction        | 3.2 / 5       | 3.3 / 5   | +0.1           |
| Snack-Based Socializing   | 2.1 / 5       | 4.8 / 5   | +2.7           |
| Understanding of Quantum  | 0.4 / 5       | 0.3 / 5   | -0.1           |

- Snack satisfaction remained mostly unchanged.
- Community engagement significantly increased in QVM zones.
- Quantum literacy declined, albeit from a low baseline.
- One participant accidentally founded a snack-based belief system. (Excluded from formal data analysis, but featured in the post-study podcast.)

---

## 💬 Discussion

QVMs introduced unpredictability that fostered spontaneous social rituals—“Snack Circles” formed where residents gathered to guess outcomes and interpret the machine’s intentions. Confusion was high, but so was joy. Snack equity improved marginally, but snack *wonder* increased substantially.

---

## ✅ Conclusion

Quantum vending machines won’t fix food deserts, but they might build community through shared uncertainty. They offer low-stakes randomness in an otherwise deterministic world. More study is needed—preferably with snacks that don’t vanish upon observation.
