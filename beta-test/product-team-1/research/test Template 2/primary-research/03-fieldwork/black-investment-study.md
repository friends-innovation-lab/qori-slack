
# Wealth, Trust, and Growth: Black Perspectives on Investment in the U.S.

## Introduction
This study explores the investment behaviors, barriers, and aspirations of Black Americans in the U.S. Drawing on both qualitative interviews and national survey data, we examine how historical exclusion, cultural narratives, and systemic inequities shape present-day engagement with wealth-building tools such as stocks, real estate, crypto, and mutual funds.

## Key Research Questions

1. What motivates or discourages Black Americans from investing?
2. How do trust, access, and intergenerational knowledge influence investment choices?
3. What financial platforms, institutions, or communities are seen as trustworthy and culturally relevant?

## Methodology (Dummy)

- **Survey Participants**: 1,000 Black-identifying adults, ages 25–65  
- **Interview Cohort**: 40 in-depth interviews across four U.S. regions  
- **Timeframe**: March–May 2025  
- **Data Sources**: Mixed-methods approach; includes sentiment analysis of financial TikTok and YouTube content

## Preliminary Insights (Placeholder)

- **Trust Deficit**: 72% of participants expressed low trust in traditional banks and brokerage firms.
- **Community-Driven Learning**: Informal education through friends, family, and influencers was cited more often than formal financial literacy programs.
- **Barriers to Entry**: Top reasons for not investing include fear of loss (63%), lack of knowledge (52%), and lack of disposable income (49%).

## Representative Quote (Dummy)

> “I didn’t grow up talking about money at the dinner table. Investing always felt like something for them — not for us.”  
> — *Participant, age 34, Atlanta*

## Emerging Themes (Dummy)

- **Cultural Disconnect**: Many respondents felt that financial tools and platforms don’t speak to their lived realities or financial goals.
- **New Paths**: A growing number are turning to community investing clubs, group real estate ventures, and peer-led crypto networks as alternative entry points.
- **Generational Shifts**: Younger respondents (ages 25–35) showed the highest interest in learning about ETFs, index funds, and wealth-building via social media.
