# Research Plan — VA Health and Benefits Mobile App Navigation Research

📦 **Product Area:** VA Mobile App — Provides Veterans with streamlined access to health records, appointment scheduling, disability claims tracking, and benefit information through an intuitive mobile interface that consolidates essential VA services into task-focused workflows.

👩🏽‍🔬 **Lead Researcher:** Taylor Kim · VA Mobile Development Team

🗓️ **Date:** February 10, 2026 · **Timeline:** standard

## 🔎 Background

This research will inform the VA product team's decision on which navigation improvements to prioritize for the mobile app redesign in Q1 2026, specifically focusing on main menu restructuring, search functionality enhancements, and information hierarchy changes. The findings will help Veterans complete health and benefits tasks more efficiently by creating clearer pathways to essential services like appointment scheduling, prescription refills, and disability claim tracking. For VA, these navigation improvements will reduce user support requests, increase app engagement, and help more Veterans successfully access their benefits through the mobile platform.

## 🧭 User Journey

**User Journey Connection:** The VA Mobile App's navigation structure, information architecture, search functionality, and task completion flows serve as a central digital gateway that supports veterans throughout their entire journey by providing streamlined access to health and benefits services across all life phases.

**Key Moments/Phases:**
- **Taking care of myself:** The mobile app's health-focused navigation allows veterans to quickly access medical records, schedule appointments, refill prescriptions, and manage their healthcare through intuitive task flows, while the search functionality helps them find specific health information or services without navigating complex menu structures.
- **Putting down roots:** The benefits-focused sections of the app support veterans transitioning to civilian life by providing easy access to disability claims status, education benefits, home loan information, and career services through streamlined task completion flows that reduce the complexity of managing multiple benefit programs simultaneously.
- **Retiring:** The app's information architecture accommodates aging veterans by organizing retirement-related benefits, pension information, and long-term care options in accessible formats, while the search functionality helps them quickly locate age-specific services and the simplified task flows reduce cognitive burden for completing essential transactions.

**Critical Pain Points:** 
- Complex information architecture that doesn't align with veterans' mental models, causing confusion when trying to locate services that span both health and benefits categories
- Search functionality that returns irrelevant results or fails to surface the most commonly needed services, forcing veterans to navigate through multiple menu levels
- Inconsistent task completion flows between different features that require veterans to learn multiple interaction patterns, creating friction especially for users with disabilities or limited digital literacy

**Supporting Goals:**
- Conduct card sorting and usability testing to map veteran mental models against the current VA Mobile App - main navigation structure, information architecture, search functionality, and task completion flows across health and benefits features, identifying the top 5 navigation pain points that cause users to abandon critical tasks like appointment scheduling, prescription refills, and disability claim status checks.

- Validate through usability testing whether proposed information architecture improvements to the VA Mobile App - main navigation structure, information architecture, search functionality, and task completion flows across health and benefits features reduce task completion time by 30% and increase successful task completion rates from 55% to 80% for the most frequently used veteran services.

## 🛠️ Methodology

**Methodology Rationale:** Card sorting reveals how veterans naturally categorize health and benefits services to optimize information architecture, while usability testing validates navigation patterns and task flows in realistic scenarios, and competitive analysis benchmarks the VA Mobile App against industry standards for government and healthcare mobile experiences.

**Implementation Approach:**

- **Session Format:** 
  - Card sorting: 60-minute hybrid sessions (30 cards representing key health/benefits features)
  - Usability testing: 90-minute moderated sessions with 8-10 core tasks
  - Competitive analysis: Structured evaluation of 6-8 government/healthcare apps using heuristic framework

- **Key Activities:**
  - Open card sorting to understand veterans' mental models for organizing appointments, prescriptions, disability claims, and benefit information
  - Task-based usability testing focusing on appointment scheduling, prescription refills, claim status checks, and document uploads
  - Search functionality evaluation through findability tasks and query analysis
  - Competitive benchmarking of navigation patterns, search interfaces, and task completion flows in Medicare, Social Security, Kaiser Permanente, and other relevant apps
  - Navigation stress testing with complex multi-step scenarios spanning health and benefits domains

- **Evaluation Protocol:**
  - Card sorting analysis using dendrograms and similarity matrices to identify optimal groupings
  - Task completion rates, error frequencies, and time-on-task measurements
  - System Usability Scale (SUS) and task-specific satisfaction ratings
  - Navigation path analysis and drop-off point identification
  - Search success rates and query refinement patterns
  - Comparative scoring matrix for competitive features against VA Mobile App capabilities

**VA Compliance Notes:**

- **Collaboration Cycle:** Requires Platform Design System team review for navigation standards alignment, 2-week stakeholder feedback period, and VFS-Platform design intent checkpoint before implementation

- **Trauma-Informed Approach:** Avoid scenarios involving mental health crises or traumatic injury claims, provide session breaks every 30 minutes, allow participants to skip uncomfortable tasks, and ensure facilitators are briefed on veteran-sensitive language and potential triggers

- **Accessibility:** Accommodate screen reader users in both card sorting (digital tools with keyboard navigation) and usability testing, provide large-print materials, offer phone-based participation options, and ensure testing environment meets ADA requirements for mobility-impaired veterans

**For Card Sorting:**
- **Card Preparation:** Create 45-60 cards representing VA Mobile App features and content including: health appointment scheduling, prescription refills, secure messaging with providers, disability claim status, benefit payment history, facility locator, telehealth video calls, lab results, immunization records, travel reimbursement, education benefits, home loan status, and profile management. Include both primary features and sub-features to test hierarchical relationships within VA health and benefits services.

- **Sorting Methodology:** Conduct hybrid card sorting sessions combining open and closed approaches. Begin with open sorting allowing participants to create their own categories for VA services, then follow with closed sorting using predetermined categories (Health Care, Benefits & Claims, Account & Profile, Support & Resources). Use both physical cards for in-person sessions and digital tools like OptimalSort for remote veteran participants.

- **Analysis Procedures:** Calculate similarity matrices to identify which VA features participants consistently group together. Generate dendrograms to visualize hierarchical relationships between health and benefits services. Use standardized distance measures and cluster analysis to determine optimal navigation groupings. Analyze agreement scores between participants to validate information architecture decisions for the VA Mobile App.

- **Results Interpretation:** Transform card sorting data into navigation structure recommendations by identifying strong feature clusters (>70% participant agreement) for primary navigation categories. Map secondary navigation based on moderate clustering patterns (50-70% agreement). Create wireframes showing proposed information architecture with clear pathways between related VA health and benefits features. Develop task flow recommendations based on how participants mentally model relationships between services like scheduling appointments and accessing medical records.

**For Usability Testing:**
- **Task Scenarios:** 
  1. You need to schedule a primary care appointment at your local VA medical center within the next two weeks and want to see available time slots before confirming your appointment.
  2. Your doctor mentioned you have new lab results available. Find and review your most recent blood work results, then send a secure message to your provider asking a follow-up question about the findings.
  3. You're expecting your disability compensation payment but want to verify the amount and payment date. Check your benefit payment history and update your direct deposit information.
  4. You need to attend a mental health appointment via telehealth next Tuesday. Locate your upcoming telehealth appointment and test your video connection to ensure it works properly.

- **Think-Aloud Protocol:** Instruct participants to verbalize their thought process while navigating the VA Mobile App, specifically noting when they feel confused about where to find features, when navigation labels are unclear, or when they expect certain functions to be grouped differently. Prompt with "What are you thinking right now?" and "What would you expect to happen next?" when participants go silent during critical navigation moments.

- **Data Collection:** Record screen interactions using tools like Lookback or UserTesting while simultaneously capturing audio commentary. Document navigation paths, time-on-task for each scenario, error recovery attempts, and instances where participants use search versus browsing. Create observation templates tracking successful task completion, points of confusion in information architecture, and user satisfaction with task flows across health and benefits features.

- **Post-Session Interviews:** Ask participants to compare the VA Mobile App navigation to other healthcare or government apps they use, identify which VA features they would use most frequently and whether those are easily accessible, describe their mental model of how VA health and benefits services should be organized, and provide feedback on search functionality effectiveness for finding specific VA services and information.

**For Competitive Analysis:**
- **Evaluation Framework:** Develop feature comparison matrices analyzing navigation structures of healthcare apps (MyChart, Kaiser Permanente, Teladoc), government service apps (Social Security, IRS2Go, USAA), and integrated health-benefits platforms. Evaluate each competitor across dimensions including: primary navigation categories, information architecture depth, search functionality, task completion flows, and integration between related services. Apply Nielsen's usability heuristics specifically focusing on navigation consistency, user control, and error prevention.

- **Documentation Standards:** Capture annotated screenshots of competitor navigation structures, document complete user flows for equivalent tasks (appointment scheduling, secure messaging, benefit checking, telehealth access), and record interaction patterns including gesture requirements, loading states, and error handling. Create standardized templates documenting navigation hierarchy, labeling conventions, and visual design patterns for each competitive app.

- **Analysis Criteria:** Identify navigation strengths such as intuitive categorization of services, effective search and filtering capabilities, seamless transitions between related tasks, and clear visual hierarchy. Document weaknesses including confusing information architecture, poor search results, broken task flows, and inconsistent navigation patterns. Extract best practices for organizing complex health and benefits information, implementing effective search functionality, and designing efficient task completion flows.

- **Synthesis Approach:** Create opportunity matrices showing where VA Mobile App can differentiate from competitors while adopting proven navigation patterns. Develop recommendations for information architecture improvements based on successful competitor approaches to organizing health and benefits services. Propose search functionality enhancements and task flow optimizations that leverage competitive insights while addressing unique VA user needs for integrated health and benefits management.

## ⚠️ Risk Management

> [!WARNING]
> **High-Risk Areas Requiring Immediate Mitigation**

> [!CAUTION] 
> **Medium-Risk Areas to Monitor Closely**

## Top 4 Risks for VA Mobile App Research

**Recruitment Challenges:** Difficulty finding Veterans who meet the dual criteria of regular VA mobile app usage AND 50% accessibility needs representation, particularly those with assistive technology experience who can effectively evaluate navigation structure and search functionality across health/benefits features.
- **Likelihood:** High
- **Mitigation:** Partner with VA disability services offices and veteran accessibility advocacy groups; offer flexible participation options (remote/in-person); extend recruitment timeline by 2 weeks; create tiered incentive structure; maintain backup pool of secondary participants (struggled users) who can provide valuable friction insights.

**Execution Issues:** Card sorting categories may not align with Veterans' mental models of health vs. benefits tasks, leading to inconclusive information architecture recommendations, especially when testing complex multi-step flows like prescription refills or disability claim status checks.
- **Likelihood:** Medium  
- **Mitigation:** Conduct hybrid card sorting with both open and closed approaches; pre-validate card categories through brief Veteran interviews; ensure usability testing scenarios mirror real-world task sequences; use think-aloud protocols during competitive analysis to capture reasoning behind navigation preferences.

**Timeline Risks:** Usability testing of task completion flows across both health AND benefits features requires extensive scenario development and longer session durations (90+ minutes), potentially causing participant fatigue and delayed analysis that could impact upcoming VA mobile app release cycles.
- **Likelihood:** Medium
- **Mitigation:** Split testing into two focused 60-minute sessions per participant (health-focused vs. benefits-focused); prioritize critical user journeys first; prepare streamlined analysis templates specific to navigation and search patterns; build 1-week buffer into final recommendations delivery.

**Quality Concerns:** Participants' varying levels of smartphone proficiency and different assistive technologies may create inconsistent feedback on search functionality and information architecture, making it difficult to distinguish between usability issues vs. technology adaptation challenges.
- **Likelihood:** Medium
- **Mitigation:** Standardize device setup and assistive technology configurations; include technology comfort assessment in screener; pair observational data with post-task confidence ratings; triangulate findings across all three research methods; conduct follow-up validation interviews with 20% of participants to confirm interpretation accuracy.
