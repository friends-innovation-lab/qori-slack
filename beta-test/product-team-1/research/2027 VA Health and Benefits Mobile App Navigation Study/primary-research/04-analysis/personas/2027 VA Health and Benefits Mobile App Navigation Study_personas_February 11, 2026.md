## Persona 1: The Accessibility-First Navigator

**Based on:** PT001, PT003

> "Every button needs a proper label. I shouldn't have to guess what things do." — PT001

**Who they are:**

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Background** | Military veterans with 12-22 years service, ages 50-62, using assistive technology due to combat injuries or age-related changes |
| **Tech Setup** | iPhone with VoiceOver or large text settings, reading glasses, some fine motor control challenges |
| **VA Usage** | Regular users for health appointments, payments, and secure messaging |

**What they're trying to do:**

- Navigate the app using screen readers or large text settings — *Evidence: PT001, PT003*
- Complete routine tasks like checking appointments and payments independently — *Evidence: PT001, PT003*
- Update contact information and manage health records — *Evidence: PT001, PT003*

**What blocks them:**

| Frustration | Evidence |
|-------------|----------|
| Unlabeled buttons announced only as "Button" by screen readers | PT001: "There's a button that just says 'Button' without any descriptive text. That's not helpful at all." |
| Inconsistent text scaling across interface elements | PT003: "Text is still too small in some places even with the large text setting." |
| Touch targets too small for comfortable interaction | PT003: "I need bigger buttons. These are hard to tap with my fingers." |
| Critical interface elements hidden at bottom of screens | PT001: Nearly missed edit button during contact information update task |

**How they cope:** Use systematic VoiceOver navigation strategies, move slowly and deliberately through interfaces, rely on fingerprint authentication to avoid typing passwords

**Design implication:** Implement comprehensive accessibility standards with proper button labeling, consistent text scaling, and larger touch targets positioned prominently in the interface hierarchy.

## Persona 2: The Efficiency-Driven Power User

**Based on:** PT002, PT003

> "Why is claims under Benefits and not on the main screen? I check that every day." — PT002

**Who they are:**

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Background** | Veterans with 8-22 years service, daily app users who rely on VA services for critical information |
| **Tech Setup** | Standard smartphone usage, good technical skills, high expectations for app performance |
| **VA Usage** | Daily usage for claims status, appointments, and payments; expects consumer-grade app experience |

**What they're trying to do:**

- Check disability claims status daily without excessive navigation — *Evidence: PT002*
- Access appointment information and payment schedules quickly — *Evidence: PT002, PT003*
- Find specific information through search functionality — *Evidence: PT002*

**What blocks them:**

| Frustration | Evidence |
|-------------|----------|
| Frequently-used features buried in deep navigation | PT002: "I had to tap like five times to get to my claim status." |
| Search functionality fails to return useful results | PT002: "The search doesn't find what I'm looking for. I typed 'claim' and got nothing useful." |
| Session timeouts interrupt active tasks | PT003: "Why does this keep logging me out? I was just using it an hour ago." |
| Confusing technical terminology without plain language explanations | PT002: "I don't understand what 'Evidence gathering' means. Is that good or bad?" |

**How they cope:** Navigate manually when search fails, check the app multiple times daily, express frustration with inefficient workflows but continue using the app for critical tasks

**Design implication:** Elevate frequently-used features to the home screen with customizable widgets, improve search functionality, and replace technical jargon with plain language explanations.

## Methodology

**Framework:** Evidence-Based Persona Development

**Approach:** Composite archetypes from behavioral patterns across participants — not 1:1 mapping.

**Data Sources:** PT001, PT002, PT003

**Why these groupings:** PT001 and PT003 both demonstrated accessibility needs (screen reader usage and large text requirements), while PT002 and PT003 both showed patterns of daily usage with efficiency expectations. The personas represent overlapping but distinct user needs within the veteran population using VA mobile services.
