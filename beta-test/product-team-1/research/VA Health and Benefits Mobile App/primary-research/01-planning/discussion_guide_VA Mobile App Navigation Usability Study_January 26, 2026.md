# 💬 Discussion Guide — VA Mobile App Navigation Usability Study

**Session Length:** 60 minutes

**Participants:** Veterans who actively use the VA mobile app at least 2-3 times monthly for health and benefits management. Mixed demographics including age ranges 25-65+, various service branches, and different geographic locations (urban/suburban/rural). Technology comfort levels from basic to advanced. Minimum 50% of participants must use assistive technologies (VoiceOver, TalkBack, large text settings) or have documented accessibility needs (vision, motor, cognitive). Include both iOS and Android users

**Date Created:** January 26, 2026

**Testing URL:** https://mobile.va.gov/app-testing/navigation-study

## 🎯 Research Objectives

1. **Evaluate navigation efficiency and user preference between bottom tab and hamburger menu patterns** by measuring task completion times, error rates, and user satisfaction scores across both designs when veterans attempt to complete core tasks (prescription refills, appointment scheduling, benefits status checks).

2. **Identify and categorize the primary usability barriers** that cause task abandonment by conducting usability testing sessions to document specific failure points, error messages, and navigation dead-ends that lead to the current 45% abandonment rate.

3. **Map veterans' mental models for organizing health and benefits information** through card sorting exercises and tree testing to understand how veterans naturally categorize and expect to find VA services, informing the optimal information architecture for the redesign.

4. **Assess accessibility compliance and navigation success rates** for veterans using assistive technologies (screen readers, voice control, large text) by testing critical user flows and measuring completion rates, time-on-task, and error frequency compared to standard navigation methods.

5. **Document the specific triggers and decision points** that cause veterans to abandon digital tasks in favor of phone support by analyzing user behavior patterns, conducting exit interviews during task abandonment, and identifying the exact moments when users lose confidence in the app's ability to meet their needs.

## 🗣️ Warm-up Questions

1. **Tell me about your military service - which branch did you serve in and what drew you to that particular branch?**
   - *Why this matters:* Builds rapport through shared respect for service while gathering demographic context. Helps establish the participant's comfort level and gets them talking about familiar territory before diving into technology topics.

2. **What does a typical day look like for you now, and how do VA services fit into your routine?**
   - *Why this matters:* Reveals real-world context for app usage patterns and identifies when/where veterans are most likely to access VA services. This helps understand the environmental and situational factors that may impact their mobile app experience.

3. **When you need to get something done quickly on your phone or tablet, what kinds of things make that experience smooth versus frustrating for you?**
   - *Why this matters:* Uncovers individual accessibility needs and technology preferences without directly asking about disabilities. Reveals mental models around efficient mobile interactions and sets up discussion of navigation expectations.

4. **Think about the last time you needed to handle something VA-related - walk me through what that was and how you went about taking care of it.**
   - *Why this matters:* Provides concrete examples of recent VA interactions and decision-making processes. Shows whether veterans default to mobile, web, phone, or in-person channels and why, revealing current pain points and workarounds.

5. **What's one thing about using technology or apps that you wish worked better for you personally?**
   - *Why this matters:* Opens discussion about individual accessibility needs and technology frustrations in a non-clinical way. Helps researchers understand each participant's specific challenges before observing them navigate the VA app, ensuring appropriate accommodations during testing.

## 🎯 Main Research Tasks

### Task 1: Prescription Refill Request
**Time:** 8 minutes

**Scenario:** 
You've been taking medication for your back pain, and you noticed this morning that you only have about 3 pills left. You need to request a refill before you run out completely.

**Instruction to Participant:**
"Please show me how you would request a refill for a prescription that's running low. Think aloud as you navigate through the app, and let me know what you're thinking at each step."

**Success Criteria:**
- Participant locates the prescription/pharmacy section
- Successfully initiates a refill request process
- Completes the refill request or reaches the final confirmation screen

**What to Observe:**
- Initial navigation path chosen (search, menu, tabs, etc.)
- Hesitation points or confusion about where to find prescriptions
- Whether they use search vs. browsing navigation
- Accessibility tool interactions (screen reader announcements, zoom gestures)
- Signs of frustration or consideration of abandoning the task

**Probing Questions:**
- "Where did you expect to find prescription information when you first opened the app?"
- "What would you do if you couldn't find this feature easily?"
- "How does this compare to how you typically handle prescription refills?"

---

### Task 2: Schedule a Routine Medical Appointment
**Time:** 10 minutes

**Scenario:** 
It's been about 6 months since your last primary care visit, and you'd like to schedule a routine check-up appointment with your doctor at your usual VA medical center for sometime in the next 3-4 weeks.

**Instruction to Participant:**
"Please walk me through how you would schedule this appointment using the app. Continue until you feel like you've successfully scheduled it or until you would normally give up and try a different method."

**Success Criteria:**
- Participant finds the appointment scheduling feature
- Navigates through appointment type selection
- Reaches provider/date selection screens
- Completes scheduling process or reaches confirmation step

**What to Observe:**
- Path taken to find appointment scheduling
- Understanding of appointment categories/types
- Interaction with date/time selection interfaces
- Accessibility of calendar and form elements
- Points where participant considers alternative methods (calling, website)

**Probing Questions:**
- "How would you organize health-related features if you were designing this app?"
- "What made that step easy or difficult?"
- "At what point, if any, would you switch to calling instead?"

---

### Task 3: Check Disability Benefits Payment Status
**Time:** 7 minutes

**Scenario:** 
You normally receive your disability compensation payment around the 1st of each month, but it's now the 5th and you haven't seen it in your bank account yet. You want to check if there are any issues with your payment.

**Instruction to Participant:**
"Please show me how you would check on the status of your disability payment using this app. Let me know what information you're looking for and whether you're finding what you need."

**Success Criteria:**
- Participant locates benefits or payments section
- Finds payment history or status information
- Accesses current month's payment details

**What to Observe:**
- Mental model of where benefits information should be located
- Distinction between health and benefits features in navigation
- Ability to interpret payment status information
- Use of search vs. browsing to find benefits features
- Accessibility of financial/tabular information

**Probing Questions:**
- "Where would you naturally look for benefits information versus health information?"
- "What other benefits information would you expect to find in this same area?"
- "How clear is the payment information that's displayed here?"

---

### Task 4: Find Your Upcoming Appointments and Medical Records
**Time:** 9 minutes

**Scenario:** 
You have a specialist appointment coming up next week, but you can't remember the exact time or which doctor it's with. You also want to review your recent lab results before the appointment so you can discuss them with your doctor.

**Instruction to Participant:**
"Please find your upcoming appointment details and then locate your recent lab results. Show me how you would access both pieces of information, and let me know if you can find everything you need."

**Success Criteria:**
- Participant locates upcoming appointments section
- Successfully views appointment details
- Finds and accesses medical records or lab results
- Can navigate between both features

**What to Observe:**
- Whether participant expects these features to be connected or separate
- Navigation patterns between related health information
- Ability to switch between different types of health data
- Screen reader navigation through appointment and medical data
- Signs of cognitive load or information overload

**Probing Questions:**
- "Do you think appointment information and medical records should be in the same section or separate areas?"
- "What would make it easier to prepare for an upcoming appointment using this app?"
- "How do you typically keep track of your VA appointments and health information?"

## 💭 Post-Session Interview

## Post-Session Interview Questions

1. **What was your overall impression of navigating the VA Health and Benefits mobile app today?**
   - *Follow-up if needed:* How did it compare to your expectations going into this session?

2. **I noticed you [specific behavior observed - e.g., used search multiple times, hesitated at certain screens, went back and forth between sections]. Can you tell me what was going through your mind during those moments?**
   - *Follow-up if needed:* What were you looking for that you couldn't find easily?

3. **When you think about organizing your health and benefits information, how do you naturally group or categorize these services in your mind?**
   - *Follow-up if needed:* Where would you expect to find prescription refills, appointment scheduling, and benefits status within an app structure?

4. **During the tasks, were there any moments where you felt frustrated or wanted to give up and call the VA instead?**
   - *Follow-up if needed:* What specifically triggered that feeling? What would have kept you engaged with the app?

5. **How did the navigation style (bottom tabs/hamburger menu) work for you? Did it help or hinder your ability to move around the app?**
   - *Follow-up if needed:* What navigation approach would feel most natural to you for accessing VA services?

6. **[If applicable] I noticed you're using [accessibility feature - screen reader, large text, etc.]. How well did the app support your needs during navigation?**
   - *Follow-up if needed:* What barriers did you encounter that made it harder to complete tasks?

7. **If you could redesign this app's organization and navigation, what would you change first?**
   - *Follow-up if needed:* What would make the most difference in helping you complete tasks successfully?

8. **On a scale of 1-10, how likely would you be to use this app for your VA health and benefits needs? What would make it a 10?**

## 🔍 Research Synthesis Prompts

**After Each Session:**

1. **What patterns am I seeing in how this veteran mentally groups health vs. benefits tasks, and does their expected navigation path align with our current information architecture?** Look for recurring themes in where they expect to find prescription refills, appointment scheduling, and benefits status - and note any disconnect between their mental model and the app's current structure.

2. **What surprised me about this veteran's interaction with navigation elements (bottom tabs, hamburger menu, search behavior), and what accessibility or usability barriers emerged that I hadn't anticipated?** Pay attention to unexpected workarounds, moments where they bypassed intended navigation paths, or accessibility challenges that created unique friction points.

3. **At what specific moments did this veteran show signs of potential task abandonment, and what underlying navigation or information architecture issues need deeper investigation in future sessions?** Document the exact points where frustration peaked, when they considered giving up, or when they mentioned they'd "just call instead."

**After All Sessions:**

4. **What are the strongest patterns across veterans' mental models for organizing health and benefits information, and which navigation approach (bottom tabs vs. hamburger menu) consistently enabled faster task completion across different accessibility needs?** Synthesize the most common expectations for information grouping and identify which navigation patterns worked best for veterans with varying abilities and tech comfort levels.

5. **How confident am I that our findings explain the current 45% task abandonment rate, and do I have enough evidence to recommend specific information architecture changes that will meaningfully improve the 4.2/10 satisfaction rating for the Q1 2026 redesign?** Assess whether the research uncovered the root causes behind the poor metrics and if the insights are robust enough to guide major redesign decisions.
