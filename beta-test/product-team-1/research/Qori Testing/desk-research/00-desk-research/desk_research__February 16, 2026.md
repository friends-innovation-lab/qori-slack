## 📊 Executive Summary

### Overview

This research examines the usability challenges older Veterans face when using VA Video Connect (VVC) telehealth platform. The mixed-methods study reveals significant barriers to successful telehealth participation among Veterans aged 65 and older, highlighting critical design flaws that impede healthcare access for this population.

### Key Findings

> **Finding 1: Poor Overall Usability Performance**
> 
> The VA Video Connect platform scored 58.4 on the System Usability Scale, falling below the acceptable threshold of 68, with performance declining significantly by age group (65-74: 64.2, 75-84: 52.8, 85+: 48.1).
> 
> *Source: JMIR_Older_Veterans_Telehealth_Usability_2024.md*

> **Finding 2: Critical Failure in Technical Problem-Solving**
> 
> Only 31% of participants successfully completed troubleshooting tasks when encountering technical issues, despite troubleshooting being essential for real-world telehealth use.
> 
> *Source: JMIR_Older_Veterans_Telehealth_Usability_2024.md*

> **Finding 3: Confusion from Multiple Communication Channels**
> 
> Veterans received appointment information through multiple inconsistent channels (text, email, MyHealtheVet), creating confusion about which access point to use for their telehealth appointments.
> 
> *Source: JMIR_Older_Veterans_Telehealth_Usability_2024.md*

> **Finding 4: High Cognitive Load Barriers**
> 
> The multi-step joining process requiring password management, app switching, and camera permissions created significant cognitive burden, with participants reporting exhaustion before appointments even began.
> 
> *Source: JMIR_Older_Veterans_Telehealth_Usability_2024.md*

> **Finding 5: Strong Preference for Human Support**
> 
> Participants expressed strong desire for accessible human technical support over automated troubleshooting, particularly when encountering technical difficulties during the telehealth joining process.
> 
> *Source: JMIR_Older_Veterans_Telehealth_Usability_2024.md*

### Strategic Implications

These findings indicate urgent need for telehealth platform redesign to serve the aging Veteran population effectively. The research provides specific recommendations including unified entry points, progressive disclosure design, and integrated human support that could significantly improve healthcare access for older Veterans.

## 🎯 Key Themes

### Theme 1: Age-Related Digital Divide in Healthcare Technology

#### What the Research Shows

Older adults face significantly greater challenges with telehealth technology adoption, with performance declining markedly across age groups. The study reveals a clear pattern where the oldest users struggle most with digital health platforms, particularly those requiring assistive technologies.

#### Supporting Evidence

> "SUS Scores by Subgroup: Overall mean: 58.4 (SD: 18.2) Age 65-74: 64.2 Age 75-84: 52.8 Age 85+: 48.1 Assistive technology users: 41.3"
> 
> — *JMIR_Older_Veterans_Telehealth_Usability_2024.md*

> "Veterans represent a unique population for telehealth research. The median age of VA healthcare users is 65.4 years, compared to 38.4 years for the general US population"
> 
> — *JMIR_Older_Veterans_Telehealth_Usability_2024.md*

#### Implications for Healthcare Technology Design

This theme suggests that healthcare systems must prioritize age-inclusive design principles and recognize that one-size-fits-all digital solutions will systematically exclude the most vulnerable older populations who may need healthcare services most.

#### Confidence: `HIGH`

Strong quantitative evidence shows consistent performance degradation across age groups with specific measurable outcomes.

### Theme 2: Cognitive Overload from Complex Multi-Step Processes

#### What the Research Shows

Current telehealth platforms impose excessive cognitive burden through fragmented, multi-step joining processes that overwhelm older users. The research demonstrates that task complexity directly correlates with failure rates, particularly for troubleshooting scenarios.

#### Supporting Evidence

> "The multi-step joining process across different interfaces created significant cognitive burden... 'I have to remember my VA password, then switch apps, then allow the camera... by the time I get in, I'm already exhausted.' — Participant 19, age 76"
> 
> — *JMIR_Older_Veterans_Telehealth_Usability_2024.md*

> "Task Completion Rates: Finding appointment link: 94% success, Testing audio/video: 71% success, Joining waiting room: 67% success, Troubleshooting connection issues: 31% success"
> 
> — *JMIR_Older_Veterans_Telehealth_Usability_2024.md*

#### Implications for System Design

Healthcare technology must prioritize simplified, linear workflows that minimize context-switching and cognitive load, especially for complex troubleshooting scenarios where users are most likely to abandon the process.

#### Confidence: `HIGH`

Clear quantitative data showing declining success rates with task complexity, supported by specific user testimony about cognitive burden.

### Theme 3: Technology Anxiety and Fear of Irreversible Mistakes

#### What the Research Shows

Older users experience significant anxiety about making irreversible errors when using telehealth platforms, leading to hesitant behavior that impedes successful task completion. This "click hesitancy" represents a major barrier to effective telehealth adoption.

#### Supporting Evidence

> "Many participants exhibited 'click hesitancy,' pausing extensively before interactions due to fear of negative consequences."
> 
> — *JMIR_Older_Veterans_Telehealth_Usability_2024.md*

> "'I was afraid if I clicked the wrong thing, I'd miss my appointment or mess something up that I couldn't fix.' — Participant 31, age 78"
> 
> — *JMIR_Older_Veterans_Telehealth_Usability_2024.md*

#### Implications for User Experience Design

Technology interfaces must build user confidence through clear feedback, undo options, and reassuring messaging that reduces anxiety about making mistakes during critical healthcare interactions.

#### Confidence: `HIGH`

Consistent qualitative evidence supported by observed behavioral patterns during usability testing.

> "Qualitative themes included: confusion about multiple entry points, anxiety about 'doing something wrong,' and desire for human support options."

> — *JMIR_Older_Veterans_Telehealth_Usability_2024.md*

#### Implications for Healthcare Service Delivery

Healthcare systems must integrate accessible human technical support as a core component of telehealth services, not an afterthought, to ensure equitable access for older populations.

#### Confidence: `HIGH`

Strong qualitative evidence from multiple participants expressing consistent preference for human over automated support.

### Theme 5: Information Fragmentation Across Multiple Channels

#### What the Research Shows

Current telehealth systems create confusion by distributing appointment information across multiple channels with inconsistent formatting and instructions. This fragmentation significantly impairs users' ability to successfully access their appointments.

#### Supporting Evidence

> "'I got a text, an email, and something in MyHealtheVet. They all had different links. I didn't know which one to click.' — Participant 12, age 71"
> 
> — *JMIR_Older_Veterans_Telehealth_Usability_2024.md*

> "Participants described receiving appointment information through multiple channels with inconsistent formatting and instructions."
> 
> — *JMIR_Older_Veterans_Telehealth_Usability_2024.md*

#### Implications for Healthcare Communication Systems

Healthcare organizations must consolidate appointment access through unified, consistent channels to reduce confusion and improve successful telehealth participation rates among older adults.
