## 🔎 Background

This research aims to inform the decision on implementing a new mental health program for Veterans. By evaluating the effectiveness of different treatment modalities, this research will help improve mental health outcomes and overall well-being for Veterans. The successful implementation of this program will not only benefit Veterans by providing them with better mental health support, but also help VA improve its services and reputation in caring for Veterans' mental health needs.

## 🛠️ Methodology

**Methodology Rationale:** Usability research is specifically right for research on research because it allows for a systematic evaluation of how users interact with research materials and tools, identifying pain points, preferences, and areas for improvement.

**Implementation Approach:**
- Session format and duration appropriate for usability: Conducting individual usability testing sessions with researchers to observe their interactions with research tools and materials, typically lasting 60-90 minutes.
- Key activities and data collection techniques specific to research: Task-based scenarios to simulate real-world research tasks, thinking aloud protocols to capture researchers' thought processes, and post-task interviews to gather feedback.
- Specific protocols for evaluating research user experience: Using standardized usability metrics such as task completion rates, time on task, and user satisfaction ratings to evaluate the effectiveness and efficiency of research tools.

**VA Compliance Notes:**
- Collaboration Cycle requirements and timeline: Ensuring that all usability research activities align with the VA's Collaboration Cycle requirements and are completed within the specified timeline.
- Trauma-informed research considerations for veteran participants: Implementing trauma-informed practices in recruitment, consent processes, and data collection to create a safe and supportive environment for veteran participants.
- Accessibility accommodations for diverse participant needs: Providing accommodations such as assistive technologies, alternative formats, and flexible scheduling to meet the diverse accessibility needs of participants.

By using usability research methods tailored to research on research, we can gain valuable insights into how researchers interact with tools and materials, identify areas for improvement, and ultimately enhance the user experience for those conducting research within the VA system.

**For Usability Testing:**
- Task scenarios: 
1. Navigate to the checkout page and complete a purchase.
2. Search for a specific product and add it to the cart.
3. Update your account information in the settings menu.

- Think-aloud protocol: Instruct participants to verbalize their thoughts and actions as they complete each task.

- Data collection: Set up screen recording software to capture participant interactions. Use an observation notes template to record key observations and timing protocols to track task completion times.

- Post-session interviews: Ask participants about their overall experience, any difficulties they encountered, and suggestions for improvement.

**For User Interviews:**
- Interview guide structure: Begin with an introduction, cover main topics such as user habits and preferences, and use probing questions to delve deeper into specific research areas.

- Question types: Include a mix of behavioral questions (e.g., "How often do you use the product?"), attitudinal questions (e.g., "How satisfied are you with the current features?"), and contextual questions (e.g., "Can you walk me through a typical use case?").

- Recording protocols: Obtain consent for audio/video recording, ensure a quiet environment, and securely store recordings for analysis.

- Analysis approach: Use thematic analysis to identify patterns and insights, create affinity maps to visualize connections, and develop a coding framework for categorizing responses.

**For Card Sorting:**
- Card preparation: Create cards representing different features or content elements, ensuring they are clear and concise.

- Sorting methodology: Allow participants to either group cards into categories (closed sort) or create their own categories (open sort). Consider a hybrid approach for more nuanced insights.

- Analysis procedures: Use statistical measures such as cluster analysis, dendrograms, and similarity matrices to identify patterns and relationships between cards.

- Results interpretation: Translate findings into actionable recommendations by identifying common themes, grouping related cards, and prioritizing key insights.

**For Survey Research:**
- Question design: Use Likert scales for attitudinal questions, validate survey metrics through pilot testing, and include open-ended questions for qualitative insights.

- Sampling strategy: Determine target demographics based on research objectives, calculate sample size using statistical methods, and ensure a representative sample.

- Distribution methods: Select appropriate survey platforms, schedule distribution at optimal times, and send reminders to increase response rates.

- Statistical analysis: Analyze survey data using descriptive statistics to summarize responses, conduct significance testing to identify trends, and adhere to reporting standards for clear presentation of results.

**For Concept Testing:**
- Concept presentation: Present prototypes with varying levels of fidelity, set up scenarios that simulate real-world use cases, and gather feedback on usability, functionality, and aesthetics.

- Testing protocol: Have participants complete tasks related to the concept, collect feedback on their experience, and ask for preferences between different concepts.

- Measurement criteria: Evaluate usability metrics such as task completion rates and error rates, test comprehension through follow-up questions, and assess appeal through user feedback.

- Iteration framework: Incorporate feedback into concept refinement by prioritizing key insights, making iterative changes, and testing updated concepts with participants.

**For Competitive Analysis:**
- Evaluation framework: Use established heuristics (e.g., Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics) to evaluate competitor products, create feature comparison matrices to assess strengths and weaknesses.

- Documentation standards: Capture screenshots of competitor products, document interaction flows, and take detailed usability notes during evaluation.

- Analysis criteria: Identify key strengths and weaknesses of competitor products, extract best practices that could be applied to your own product, and prioritize areas for improvement.

- Synthesis approach: Synthesize competitive insights into actionable recommendations by highlighting areas for enhancement, proposing design changes based on best practices, and developing a roadmap for product development.
