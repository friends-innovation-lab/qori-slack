# Session Information
Participant: Jamal Rivers (TH004)  
Date: September 6, 2025  
Session Time: 3:30 PM – 4:30 PM EST  
Moderator: Jordan Reyes  
Observer: Taking live notes via /take-notes

Jamal joined at 3:30 PM and was confident right away with the SMS workflow. He's an Android phone user with unstable rural internet, which affects his telehealth readiness, and he relies heavily on TalkBack for navigation. He's skilled with swiping and explore-by-touch—this isn't someone new to assistive technology. He knows what he's doing, which made his frustrations throughout the session all the more telling.
By minute 2, he was navigating the text reminder smoothly. "The text reminder always works best for me." He completed the first task—locating the reminder and link—without issue. "TalkBack reads most of the message clearly." So far so good.
But around minute 9, things started breaking down during the connection test. "There are buttons that just say 'Button.'" This is a severe usability blocker for TalkBack users—unlabeled buttons mean he has no idea what action he's about to take. "I'm never sure which one starts the test." He was navigating through controls that gave him no meaningful information. "TalkBack is reading a list but none of it tells me what to tap."
By minute 16, his voice had tightened. The permission dialog lacked proper labeling. "Some permission prompts don't read right." He wasn't even sure if he had allowed the camera the first time—"I didn't know if I allowed the camera the first time." When you can't confirm what choice you made, that's a fundamental accessibility failure. Around minute 25, he paused and scanned longer as TalkBack navigated through cluttered controls. Elements were reading out of sequence and hidden controls weren't reachable without exhaustive swiping.
By minute 34, he felt relief when the test finally launched. He joined the visit simulation successfully and said "Once I'm in the waiting screen, I feel fine." The waiting room state is simple and readable. But the path to get there was filled with obstacles.
Around minute 45, frustration resurfaced with the Edit button for updating contact info. "Where's the edit button? I'm not hearing it." This is the same hidden Edit button issue we've seen with other participants, but amplified for screen reader users. The button was extremely hard to locate using TalkBack. Custom UI components are missing accessibility metadata, and important actions aren't reachable in a predictable focus order.
He ended the session around minute 53 balanced but direct—frustrated, yet constructive. Throughout, he was patient and provided practical examples from real appointments. He knows exactly what he needs: fully labeled controls, predictable focus order, and consistent gesture access to actions.
The three critical moments: First, the unlabeled buttons at minute 9—a severe blocker that makes core actions inaccessible. Second, the permission prompt ambiguity at minute 16, where Jamal couldn't confirm what choice he'd made. Third, the hidden Edit button at minute 45, a repeated pattern across all participants that's especially damaging for screen reader users.
This session makes the strongest case yet for a full accessibility audit. TalkBack labeling needs to be corrected across the board, permission flows need redesign with proper accessibility hints, and every interactive element needs meaningful labels. The SMS reminder works, the waiting room works—but everything in between is creating unnecessary barriers for users who depend on screen readers.
