# Session Information
Participant: Sheila Torres (PT001)  
Date: September 3, 2025  
Session Time: 2:00 PM – 3:00 PM EST  
Moderator: Jordan Reyes  
Observer: Taking live notes via /take-notes

Sheila joined the session at 2:00 PM and was calm and comfortable from the start. She mentioned right away that she uses captions for all video communications, prefers her mobile phone over a laptop, and has a high appointment frequency with telehealth. She's a strong reader but sensitive to long blocks of text—something that became very apparent as the session went on.
For the first task, I asked her to locate the telehealth instructions from her appointment reminder. She found the email quickly and said "The reminder email is usually clear enough for me to skim and get what I need." But when she opened the actual instructions, her energy shifted. Around minute 19, she let out a soft laugh and said "The instructions feel long. I skim because it's overwhelming." You could hear the fatigue in her voice. She ended up skipping about half the instructions because the paragraphs were too dense. She completed the task, but only partially—she got the gist but missed details.
The second task was running the device check. The camera permission step went smoothly and she said "The camera permission step was pretty simple." But then the trouble started. Around minute 12, she became visibly frustrated because the captions were lagging behind the audio—sometimes a full sentence behind. She paused multiple times waiting for captions to catch up, and at one point said "The captions don't sync with the video instructions." This really disrupted her flow. She also didn't expect the device check to open in a browser tab. "I didn't know the device test would open in a browser. I thought it would stay in the app." That caused momentary disorientation. She lost her place while scrolling through the instructions and said "I never know what I'm supposed to do next after the device test." Another partial completion—she got through it, but with confusion along the way.
By minute 27, she had become more cautious. She was scrolling slowly through steps and expressed worry about missing something important. "I always feel like I'm missing an important step before joining." Around minute 40, there was slight relief when the camera check worked without issue.
For the third task—joining the visit simulation—things went better. She used the email link, understood the waiting screen ("The waiting screen makes sense once it loads"), and felt confident once she was inside the visit. This one she completed fully.
The fourth task was updating her contact information. She found the Profile section without much trouble, but then spent a while looking for how to actually edit it. Around minute 48, her concern resurfaced when she couldn't find the Edit button. "The 'Edit' button was at the bottom. I almost didn't see it." This confirms something we've seen before—important controls aren't visually prioritized and often get pushed below the fold. Partial completion again.
She ended the session on a positive note but emphasized that caption syncing really affects her confidence. Her core needs are clear: she wants step-by-step instructions in short bursts or simple lists, not dense paragraphs. She needs reliable caption timing during any video content. And she needs action buttons to be high-visibility rather than buried at the bottom of the screen.
The three moments that stood out most: First, the caption lag at minute 12 that completely broke her flow and disrupted her understanding of the technical instructions. Second, her overwhelm at minute 19 when she encountered the long instruction paragraphs—a clear signal that we need simplified formats. Third, the hidden Edit button at minute 48, which confirms the inconsistent interface layouts that make basic tasks harder than they should be.
Sheila was patient, thoughtful, and very articulate about her needs throughout. She demonstrated realistic pre-appointment behavior and made a strong case for why unclear steps become stress amplifiers for users who depend on telehealth. This session gives us solid evidence for improving caption sync, simplifying prep instructions, and re-sequencing content to reduce cognitive load.
